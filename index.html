Workshop focus

Which modalities matter, and where reasoning should live

Large RGB-pretrained backbones now transfer across many tasks, and language has become a general interface for reasoning and instruction. At the same time, deployments in driving, manipulation, and industrial automation continue to rely on heterogeneous sensing that extends well beyond the visible spectrum, including depth, LiDAR, radar, thermal and other non-visible bands, tactile sensing, and proprioception.

It remains unresolved which of these modalities are necessary, which become redundant as pretraining data volumes grow, and how reasoning across them should be organized. These decisions are among the first that any robot learning system makes, and they shape data collection, pretraining, transfer, robustness, and cost. Different application domains have adopted markedly different solutions, and those solutions are rarely examined side by side.

To confront these choices directly, the program brings together invited speakers spanning autonomous driving, general robotics, and industrial automation. The workshop aims to surface where their assumptions agree, where they diverge, and what a more domain-agnostic account of modality selection and cross-modal reasoning might look like.

KITScenes LongTail Challenge

The benchmark that grounds the discussion

The challenge evaluates end-to-end driving models, including vision-language models and vision-language-action models, in rare scenarios. It assesses whether multimodal models generalize from limited examples while respecting high-level instructions, trajectory feasibility, and semantic coherence. The challenge runs before the workshop, with submission, evaluation, and a public leaderboard administered through a Hugging Face submission space. Participation is independent of paper acceptance, and winning teams are invited to give short spotlight talks.

Open questions for the community

Five contested questions

The call for papers and the closing panel are tied to these. The KITScenes LongTail Challenge keeps the discussion grounded in measurable failures.

Q1

Modality sufficiency and redundancy

When does adding a modality improve autonomy, and when does it become redundant once models are scaled and pretrained on dominant modalities? Is there a principled way to determine the minimal sufficient sensor and representation set for a target task and operating domain?

Q2

Where cross-modal reasoning should live

Should cross-modal reasoning operate through language as an explicit interlingua, through a shared learned latent space, or through modality-specific reasoning fused late? What does each choice cost in interpretability, data efficiency, and reliability?

Q3

Cross-domain transfer of modality recipes

Are the modality and reasoning requirements for autonomous driving fundamentally different from those for manipulation and industrial automation, or does a shared recipe transfer? Why do domains solving similar perception problems converge on different sensor stacks?

Q4

Robustness under degraded or missing modalities

How should a system reason when a modality is corrupted, miscalibrated, or absent at run time? Can multimodal models degrade gracefully rather than fail silently, and how should this be tested?

Q5

Evaluation beyond aggregate scores

How is the contribution of a single modality and the quality of multimodal reasoning measured beyond aggregate task scores? How far do criteria such as few-shot generalization, instruction following, semantic coherence, and trajectory quality transfer to manipulation, industrial automation, and robot learning more broadly?

Call for papers

Contributions that advance the open questions

We invite contributions addressing the workshop's five open questions, including new, preliminary, and in-progress work, position papers, and negative results. Short papers and extended abstracts are welcome, keeping the barrier low for early-stage ideas. Review criteria favor relevance to the open questions, originality, and potential to spark discussion over polish.
